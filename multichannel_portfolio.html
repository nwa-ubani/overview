Orchestration Strategy & Principles

I developed a comprehensive orchestration framework built on three core principles that would govern all cross-channel marketing decisions and ensure unified customer experiences.

01
Customer-First Routing
The customer's preferred channel and recent engagement history determines the channel, timing, and frequency of messages — not the marketing team's campaign calendar.
02
Unified Message Intelligence
Every channel knows what every other channel is doing. No duplicate messages. No contradictory offers. One cohesive story told across all touchpoints.
03
Dynamic Frequency Optimization
Engagement-based throttling automatically adjusts message frequency. Highly engaged customers hear more. Fatigued customers get breathing room.
🎯
Channel Priority Logic
Not all channels are equal for all messages. Each message type has a preferred channel hierarchy based on urgency, importance, and typical conversion rates.
Example: Cart abandonment starts with email (low friction), escalates to SMS after 4 hours if not opened, uses push only if both fail and cart value >$150.
⏱️
Timing Coordination
Messages across channels are spaced strategically. If a customer receives an email, other channels wait 4+ hours before sending related content.
Example: Customer gets promotional email at 10am. SMS about the same promo is suppressed until 2pm minimum. In-app banner only shows if email unopened after 24 hours.
🚫
Message Suppression Rules
Customers who already took action don't need reminders. Sophisticated suppression prevents post-purchase promotional messages and duplicate content.
Example: Customer purchases via email link. All promotional messages across all channels suppressed for 7 days. Cart abandonment sequences immediately terminated.
📊
Engagement-Based Cadence
Message frequency adapts to individual engagement patterns. Highly engaged customers can receive more. Disengaged customers get fewer, higher-value messages.
Example: Customer with 60%+ email open rate can receive up to 5 emails/week. Customer with <10% open rate limited to 1-2 emails/week of only highest-value content.
🎨
Creative Consistency
When the same campaign runs across channels, the creative, messaging, and offer remain consistent. Customers see one cohesive story, not conflicting narratives.
Example: "Summer Sale - 30% Off" email uses same headline, visual style, and discount code as SMS, push, and in-app versions. Only format adapts to channel.
⚡
Real-Time Attribution
Every channel tracks which touchpoint drove conversion. This data feeds back into routing logic to optimize future channel selection for each customer.
Example: Customer consistently converts via SMS but ignores email. Future campaigns prioritize SMS for this customer while reducing email frequency.

Key Learnings & Best Practices

Building true multi-channel orchestration taught us valuable lessons that shaped our approach and can guide future lifecycle marketing strategies.

🧠
Tech Consolidation is Non-Negotiable
True orchestration is impossible with 5+ disconnected tools. We had to consolidate to a single platform (Braze) that could manage all digital channels. The migration was painful but essential. Trying to "orchestrate" across disparate tools via Zapier workflows is duct tape, not infrastructure. Invest in proper tooling upfront or accept you can't truly orchestrate.
⏰
Timing Matters More Than We Thought
The gap between messages is critical. Sending email and SMS simultaneously feels efficient but kills performance. The sweet spot was 4-6 hours between channel touchpoints for most campaigns. Too fast = annoying redundancy. Too slow = missed urgency window. This timing became one of our highest-leverage optimization areas.
🎭
Not All Channels Are Created Equal
SMS has 10x the engagement of email but 100x the annoyance factor when overused. Push notifications drive immediate action but degrade quickly with frequency. In-app messaging is contextual gold but easy to overdo. We learned to treat each channel according to its unique strengths and fragility. Channel strategy isn't "use them all" — it's "use each perfectly."
🎯
Suppression Logic is the Real Magic
The wins didn't come from sending more messages — they came from sending fewer, better-timed messages. Our most powerful "campaigns" were actually suppression rules: don't message recent purchasers, don't duplicate across channels, don't interrupt high-intent moments. We spent more time on what NOT to send than what to send. That's where orchestration lives.
📊
Attribution Gets Messy Fast
With multiple channels touching customers, attribution became complicated. Email gets the click but SMS drove awareness? Push reminded but in-app closed? We implemented multi-touch attribution but also tracked "channel assist" metrics. The real insight: orchestration works as a system. Trying to credit individual channels misses the point.
👥
Organizational Alignment is Half the Battle
The tech stack was easier to fix than the org chart. Email, SMS, push, and in-app were owned by different teams with different KPIs. We had to align everyone on shared goals (customer engagement, LTV, conversion) vs channel-specific vanity metrics. Orchestration requires organizational orchestration first. One team, one goal, one customer experience.
🔄
Start Simple, Then Layer Complexity
We tried to build the perfect orchestration engine on day one and almost broke everything. The winning approach: start with basic rules (no duplicate messages within 4 hours), prove value, then add sophistication (behavioral triggers, predictive routing, dynamic frequency). Complexity without foundation fails. Foundation without iteration stagnates.
💡
Customer Preferences Trump Our Strategy
We built elaborate routing logic, but the biggest win was simply asking customers how they want to hear from us. Some people love SMS. Others hate it. Some check email religiously. Others never open it. Preference center enrollment jumped 10x when we explained "this helps us annoy you less." Respecting preferences isn't just nice — it's performance optimization.
